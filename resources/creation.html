Principles of Project Management in the Creation and in the Scriptures

Overview

Throughout the semester, my ultimate goal is to help you identify, understand, implement, and develop habits of living sound principles of effective project management. What is meant by principles? Elder Richard G. Scott provides one of the best definitions of which I know:

...search for principles. Carefully separate them from the detail used to explain them. Principles are concentrated truth, packaged for application to a wide variety of circumstances. A true principle makes decisions clear even under the most confusing and compelling circumstances. It is worth great effort to organize the truth we gather to simple statements of principle. I have tried to do that with gaining spiritual knowledge. The result is now shared in hope that it will be a beginning place for your study. (Acquiring Spiritual Knowledge, October 1993)

In preparation for our class discussion, please join me in searching for principles of project management from the accounts of one of the most significant projects that I am aware of, the creation of this earth. The bulk of these accounts are found in Genesis 1, 2, Moses 1, 3, Abraham 3, 5

You may also want to attend the temple for additional insights. Other aspects of this project can be found throughout the scriptures and in the words of modern day prophets, so don't be afraid to use the topical guide or other sources as well. Search for principles of project management that we can use in accomplishing large scale projects involving coordinated work teams. Consider the following questions to help you get started, but please don't use a question and answer format for your paper:

  • How was the need for this project identified?
  • What was the purpose or mission of this project?
  • What was the scope of the creation project (did it have a start and finish - if so what were they)?
  • How was the entire effort organized?
  • What was the organizational structure like (who reported to whom)?
  • How was the project plan created, refined, discussed, decided upon, and communicated?
  • How was the progress or status of the project monitored and communicated?
  • How was the success or failure of project tasks evaluated or approved?
  • What were phases or divisions of the project? (was there more than one layer?)
  • Were deadlines or time a factor?
  • Was there a budget or costs associated with the project?
  • Were there resources (people, tools, equipment, technology, materials, etc.) involved to achieve the project goals? You can also select and consider principles that you can learn from other projects in the scriptures such as:
  • Nephi building the ship to come to the promised land (1 Nephi 17-18)
  • Captain Moroni's "strategems" for particular battles (Alma 43-56)
  • Plans for the building the Kirtland temple (D&C 109)
  • Methods for moving the saints west (D&C 136)
  • Leading the children of Israel out of Egypt and on to Canaan (Exodus)
  • Building Solomon's temple (1 Chronicles 21, 2 Chronicles 26-27, 1 Kings 6-8)
  • Feeding the 5,000 (Matthew 14)

Deliverable 1

Please prepare a 1-page summary of at least 3 principles, patterns, practices, concepts, or ideas relating to project management you see at work in the creation of the world. Use the sample format below to organize your response.

  1. Principle/Summary - short statement in your own words
  2. Description - further explanation
  3. Support - Specific reference(s), phrase(s), verse(s), or interpretations by modern day prophets.

Example

Humility is essential to the acquiring of spiritual knowledge.

To be humble is to be teachable. Humility permits you to be tutored by the Spirit and to be taught from sources inspired by the Lord, such as the scriptures. The seeds of personal growth and understanding germinate and flourish in the fertile soil of humility. Their fruit is spiritual knowledge to guide you here and hereafter. A proud individual cannot know the things of the Spirit. Paul taught this truth, saying:
