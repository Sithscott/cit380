Project Management

CIT 380

Course Overview: Instructor

Outcomes

By the end of the semester, each student will:

  1. Be an effective communicator, team member, and leader (20%)
  2. Be proactive, able to operate in poorly defined or constrained situations (35%)
  3. Know how to create, coordinate, execute, and evaluate a project plan (20%)
  4. Be able to list and define the core principles of project management, identify and implement multiple methodologies, and explain how they can be used to make a project successful (15%)
  5. Be self-regulated, life-long learners (10%)

Course Description

This course introduces concepts, issues, approaches, tools, techniques, and technologies applicable to the management of projects. Projects can be defined as any temporary endeavor undertaken to create unique product, service, or result. The course explores how a manager can plan, organize, implement and control non-routine activities to achieve cost, schedule and performance objectives.

Read carefully the outcomes above. Notice that #2 is ranked higher than any of the other outcomes. This course is designed to help students become more proactive...to learn to "Act and not be acted upon" It is important to keep this goal in mind when approaching this course. Make sure to remind the students of this outcome often.

Three Paths introduced

This course is structured to allow students three different paths of completion. We will label these paths as 'A', 'B', and 'C'. They will decide which path they would like to pursue by the end of Week 2. Your focus during this time will be to help them to decide which path is best for them.

All projects will have a required mid-project and end of project report that students must complete. The mid report should happen no later than Week 6, the final report no later than Week 12.

Make sure that the students understand that they may complete more than one project over the course of the semester should they choose. Completing many small to medium complexity projects can often be more valuable than one large project.

Path A

Path A allows the student the most flexibility and the most opportunity to show that they can take initiative and organize and plan a good project. The deliverables, milestones, and grading are owned by the students. Students working on plan A should do the following:

  • Develop a project idea accompanied with a charter and Project Management Plan modeled off of things they have researched and studied about project management best practices.
  • Complete the proposed project as a group
  • Develop and follow a project communication plan (part of the PMP)
  • Develop an assessment plan (part of the PMP)

The Maximum grade for a Plan A student is 100%

Path B

Path B allows for students to still gain experience in becoming more proactive, but with more scaffolding to help them get started. They are given a project charter and plan template to base the planning of their selected project around. These projects can be completed as a group or individually. If done individually a Plan B student can get a maximum of 85%. If done as a group the maximum grade is 95%

Path C

Not all students are prepared to become more proactive. Plan C provides a pre-built project with charter and Project Managment Plan. The project which the students will complete is to prepare to pass the PMI PMP exam.

Because this plan does not require students to grow in the areas of outcomes one and two, the maximum grade a Plan C student can achieve is 80%.

Changing Plans

A important thing to note and mention to the students. They can switch plans! If a student begins Plan C for example...but then takes the initiative to organize a group of other Plan C students to coordinate and work together towards a common goal they are showing progress on outcomes one and two...this would qualify them for a higher plan.

Changing plans should be discussed with the professor. Either the student or instructor can initiate this conversation.

Time Consumption

The first three weeks of the semester will be the heaviest for the instructor. You will need to help the students choose a plan, project, and help advise them on putting their charters and PMPs together. You should be very active in the discussions and as available as you can be. After week three the required load on the instructor can drop significantly.
